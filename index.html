Nationalism

In vague terms, I am a left-wing nationalist, meaning I believe in the idea of the nation and its rights. My particular vein of nationalist thought is built on these five basic principles:

  • All nations (peoples/narodi) are equal before God; no any nation is innately superior or inferior to any other.
  • Wherever possible, all nations should coöperate together for humanity’s benefit.
  • Only the nation can define itself, not any outside force.
  • All nations are sovereign and have the same right to self-determination, independence, and autonomy in their own areas of majority. In Karadžić’s terms, „a sovereign nation means a nation that has power over itself, and no one can impose power upon it... Sovereign nations... can each say for themselves what they want; they cannot say this for other nations.“
  • The nation is the best basis for the state, and the state should be governed by the nation according to the nation’s interests.

People like to argue with me because they think that „nationalism“ can only refer to an ideology of one nation’s supremacy over others, but this is not how I see it. I find „nationalism“ the best way to describe my ideals of universal friendship, equality, and coöperation.

Essential to understanding my policy is the distinction between these related terms:

  • Nation: a group of people united by their common identity based in language or culture. It is closely related to ethnicity, but slightly broader, as it is not defined solely by blood.
  • State: a sovereign political entity which governs a specific populace in a specific territory
  • Country: the territory governed by a specific state.
  • Ethnicity: The cultural heritage of one’s ancestors, defined by blood.

I offer for an example Vassilis Tsitsanis, because I like him.

Tsitsanis’s ethnicity is Vlach because of his ancestry.
Tsitsanis’s nation is the Greeks, as he was fully assimilated into Greek culture, spoke the Greek language, had a Greek name, and was a Greek Orthodox Christian.
Tsitsanis’s country and state is Greece, as he was born in the Kingdom of Greece and had Greek citizenship.

Not all states are nations, and not all nations have their own state.
I categorise this with three groups:

  • Nation-states such as Poland or Finland
  • Nations without a state, such as the Romani or Kurdish peoples
  • States that are not nations, such as America or Australia

I think that national culture in general is beautiful and precious, and needs to be protected from the threats of globalism and „White“ identity. Karadžić said „Globalisation is about the melding of cultures. What does it do for us? There is nothing about maintaining tradition. Globalisation is just deculturisation! We are removing cultural content of people. Culture comes from God and the mother’s milk.“ Thus my policy is for the protection and preservation of all nations and their culture.

  • Repatriation of people to their nation-state should be strongly encouraged and supported, although never forced. For example, Serbs in America should return to Serbia.
  • Immigration from other nations should be strictly controlled. Anyone seeking to immigrate to a nation-state should fully assimilate into that nation before they are granted citizenship.
  • Travel should not be restricted unless it poses a tangible threat to the nation-state.

All people within a country are defined by one of the following four categories. Human rights must be given to all people everywhere and regardless of their categorical status.

  • Citizens of a nation-state are members of the nation, whether by birth or by assimilation thereinto. They have documents proving their citizenship, and are owed full civil rights.
  • Residents are foreign nationals who are studying or working in another country. In my humble opinion, this is beneficial for both the resident and for the country. Residents are defined by government documents proving their status as residents, and are afforded certain privileges beyond the base rights owed to all people, but they do not have full civil rights unless they become citizens.
  • Travelers are foreign nationals who are temporarily visiting another country, known by the state.
  • People who are in a country against the will of the state are trespassers and are liable to be deported. They are still owed human rights by the state.
