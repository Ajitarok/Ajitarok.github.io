Ajitarok Politics

Nationalism

In vague terms, I am a left-wing nationalist. My particular vein of nationalist thought is built on these five basic principles:

  • All nations (peoples/narodi) are equal before God; no any nation is innately superior or inferior to any other.
  • Wherever possible, all nations should coöperate together for humanity’s benefit.
  • Only the nation can define itself, not any outside force.
  • All nations are sovereign and have the same right to self-determination, independence, and autonomy in their own areas of majority. In Karadžić’s terms, „a sovereign nation means a nation that has power over itself, and no one can impose power upon it... Sovereign nations... can each say for themselves what they want; they cannot say this for other nations.“
  • The nation is the best basis for the state, and the state should be governed by the nation according to the nation’s interests.

People like to argue with me because they think that „nationalism“ can only refer to an ideology of one nation’s supremacy over others, but this is not how I see it. I find „nationalism“ the best way to describe my ideals of universal friendship, equality, and coöperation. Those critics who disagree may call my system of ideals Ajitarokite Nationalism, if they really need to.

Essential to understanding my policy is the distinction between these related terms:

  • Nation: a group of people united by their common identity based in language or culture. It is closely related to ethnicity, but slightly broader, as it is not defined solely by blood.
  • State: a sovereign political entity which governs a specific populace in a specific territory
  • Country: the territory governed by a specific state.
  • Ethnicity: The cultural heritage of one's ancestors, defined by blood.

I offer for an example Vassilis Tsitsanis, because I like him.

Tsitsanis’s ethnicity is Vlach because of his ancestry.
Tsitsanis’s nation is the Greeks, as he was fully assimilated into Greek culture, spoke the Greek language, had a Greek name, and was a Greek Orthodox Christian.
Tsitsanis’s country and state is Greece, as he was born in the Kingdom of Greece and had Greek citizenship.

To Serbo-Croatian speakers, I like to explain it this way:
„state“ directly translates to država;
„nation“ is analagous to narod;
nacija is actually more similar to „citizenry“, despite the obvious cognate;
„country“ translates to zemlja.

Not all states are nations, and not all nations have their own state.
I categorise this with three groups:

  • Nation-states such as Poland or Finland
  • Nations without a state, such as the Romani or Kurdish peoples
  • States that are not nations, such as America or Australia
